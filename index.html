K9HeatGuard by ThermaPup

Abstract

Dogs can overheat quickly, but owners often do not realize there is a problem until symptoms like heavy panting, weakness, vomiting, or collapse have already started. This is important because dogs cannot cool themselves as efficiently as humans and rely mostly on panting, meaning their body temperature can rise fast during hot walks, car rides, outdoor play, or summer heat waves.

The problem we are solving is the lack of a simple, real-time way for dog owners to know when their pet may be approaching dangerous heat levels before it becomes an emergency.

K9HeatGuard by ThermaPup is a smart wearable dog collar attachment designed to give owners an early warning when heat risk rises. Our prototype uses a thermal infrared sensor to detect temperature, an ESP32 microcontroller to process the readings, and green, yellow, and red LEDs to clearly show safe, warning, and critical temperature levels. When the temperature reaches a dangerous range, the red LED turns on, a speaker/buzzer alerts nearby people, and the system is designed to send live temperature updates and alerts to a phone app.

By combining temperature sensing, visible signals, sound alerts, and mobile notifications, K9HeatGuard helps owners act sooner by moving their dog to shade, offering water, stopping activity, or seeking veterinary care. It is not meant to replace a veterinarian, but to support faster, safer decisions when every degree matters.

Background Research & Problem Definition

Every summer, news stories remind us that dogs can overheat in minutes, yet many guardians only notice trouble when a pet is already panting heavily, stumbling, or refusing to walk. Unlike people, dogs lose heat mainly through panting and limited paw-pad sweating, so temperature can climb fast during ordinary activities: a sunny walk, agility practice, or a few minutes in a parked car. Our team studied this gap between risk rising and human awareness because early action saves lives, reduces emergency vet visits, and gives families confidence when they bring dogs into the real world.

The problem we are solving is not “knowing heat exists,” but detecting dangerous trends early and communicating them clearly to the person who can intervene. K9HeatGuard is our engineering response: a wearable path to continuous monitoring and timely alerts so owners can move a dog to shade, offer water, slow activity, or seek professional care before a crisis.

Problem Definition (Thesis Statement)

Situations where dogs are involved in heat-related emergencies are often preventable if owners receive time-accurate warnings, so we created our product, K9HeatGuard, as a real-time alerting wearable dog collar to support safer decisions under stress.

Relevant Statistics

The points below summarize widely cited veterinary guidance and physiology we used to frame safe ranges and urgency in our design (sources: veterinary health organizations and clinic education materials).

  • Cooling limitation: Dogs depend heavily on panting; they cannot regulate body temperature the way humans do with full-body sweat.
  • Typical resting range: Many clinics cite roughly 101–102.5°F as a common resting temperature band for dogs, with context depending on breed, activity, and environment.
  • Elevated risk: Sustained readings around 103°F and above warrant attention; 104°F+ is often treated as a red flag to cool the dog and contact a veterinarian, especially with symptoms such as weakness, vomiting, or collapse.
  • Time sensitivity: Heat stress can escalate quickly; rapid owner response (shade, airflow, cool—not ice-cold—water, veterinary advice) improves outcomes.

Target Market Demographics

We organized likely users so our product story, alerts, and testing scenarios stay realistic.

  • Primary households: Suburban and urban dog owners in warm seasons (including New Jersey heat waves), especially those who walk dogs daily, visit dog parks, hike, or travel by car.
  • Activity-heavy segments: Owners of working breeds, sport dogs, and “weekend athletes” whose dogs ramp up intensity quickly.
  • Secondary professionals: Trainers, groomers, sitters, and shelter staff who manage groups of dogs outdoors or in variable AC.
  • Informed allies: Veterinarians and vet-tech teams who set expectations for what wearables can and cannot replace (screening tool, not a diagnosis).

Potential User Survey Results

To ground K9HeatGuard in real needs, we collected feedback through surveys coordinated with teachers at Golda Och Academy (GOA) and veterinarians in the New Jersey area who generously shared time and expertise. Themes that rose to the top included: worry during midday walks and car transitions; desire for plain-language alerts (what is wrong, what to do next); interest in a collar-mounted option that does not get in the way of play; and repeated emphasis that any device must respect veterinary limits—clear escalation guidance rather than replacing a clinic exam.

Those responses directly shaped our alert wording, our focus on continuous monitoring over one-off checks, and our emphasis on early warning rather than “diagnosis.”

Final Engineering Design

K9HeatGuard began as a STEM project to solve dog heat risk with practical engineering.

1. Identify the problem

We found many heat emergencies happen because owners are warned too late.

We defined our problem as: “How can we give dog owners an early warning before their pet’s temperature becomes dangerous?”

2. Research & brainstorm

We studied canine heat biology, safe ranges, and existing wearables.

We chose a collar sensor because it works indoors, outdoors, and in cars.

3. Plan the solution

We sketched sensor, controller, power, and communication layout.

We also planned short, clear alert messages for stressful situations.

4. Build prototypes

We built a physical model and mock alert screens.

A full build uses a temperature sensor, ESP32, battery, and alert channel.

5. Test & improve

We tested hot-day, car, and high-activity scenarios. We asked:

  • Would the owner get the alert early enough?
  • Are the instructions in the message easy to follow?
  • Is the collar comfortable and secure for the dog?

Feedback improved alert wording and sensor placement.

6. Reflect & next steps

We identified strengths and next fixes, including broader dog-size testing.

Future versions may add app tools, GPS, and vet-facing monitoring.

Test & Reflect

This section summarizes prototype testing, user feedback, and next improvements. K9HeatGuard is an early-warning tool, not a diagnosis.

Testing Scenarios

We tested with 10 users in realistic situations to measure comfort, speed, and reliability.

  • Scenario 1 - Comfort and wearability: Dogs wore the collar while users watched fit, movement, and visible stress/discomfort.
  • Scenario 2 - Temperature accuracy: Sensor readings were checked against expected warming conditions.
  • Scenario 3 - Warning response: Rising-temperature simulations triggered alerts, and we timed how fast users noticed and reacted.

Test Results

  • Setup: 80% said the device was easy to set up and use.
  • Alert visibility: 90% rated alerts noticeable or very noticeable.
  • Reaction speed: 80% responded within 5 seconds; 100% within 10 seconds.
  • Temperature confidence: 90% rated readings mostly or extremely accurate.
  • Comfort: 60% reported comfortable; 40% reported discomfort.
  • Timing: 60% reported a slight 1-3 second delay.

Reviewer Feedback

User and reviewer comments were consistent across tests:

  • Audio works: 70% noticed sound first.
  • Comfort needs work: about 40% reported discomfort.
  • Delay matters: many users saw a 1-3 second lag.
  • Keep it simple: users want clear alerts and next steps.

Reflections and Design Iterations

The core system works: users noticed alerts fast and understood risk levels. Main gaps are comfort and small delays.

We updated enclosure and strap design from feedback. One major change was adding the AirTag holder in later iterations. CAD, drawings, and before/after photos are in Engineering Deliverables.

Future Work / Improvements

  • Make the collar smaller, lighter, and more adjustable.
  • Reduce delay so alerts feel instant.
  • Improve enclosure durability for active use.
  • Test more breeds, sizes, and environments.
  • Add user-selectable alert modes.

Marketing Plan

Position K9HeatGuard as an affordable heat-safety collar with fast alerts and simple setup.

Launch with demo videos, community showcases, trainers/dog parks, and summer campaigns. Keep messaging clear: early warning, not diagnosis.

Why testing heat risk matters

Dogs cool less efficiently than humans, so temperature can rise quickly in heat.

What we watch for in firmware

  • Normal: Green LED, buzzer off, stable safe-state behavior.
  • Elevated: Yellow LED, buzzer off, warning state in UI copy.
  • Critical: Red LED, buzzer on, sustained critical alert behavior with cooldown.
